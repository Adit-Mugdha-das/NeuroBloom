\addcontentsline{toc}{chapter}{Abstract}

NeuroBloom is a web-based, modules-based application that is used to assist in managing cognitive functioning and clinician-directed recovery of multiple sclerosis (MS) in the long-term. To address a typical lack of MS care the project was adopted: the issues of cognition have a tendency to accumulate over time and cannot be adequately evaluated with the help of occasional visits to the hospital or only one paper and pencil test. Instead of looking at cognition as an activity that can only be measured at a single point, NeuroBloom simplifies a digital task execution chain, a context-based data gathering systematized into context, time based progress and clinician follow up all in a single system.It consists of patient side cognitive assessment and training systems, physician side and executive management review systems. The cognitive task versions of the current form of NeuroBloom will be 35 questions in six domains i.e., working memory, processing speed, attention, cognitive flexibility, planning and visual scanning. A baseline testing can be used to make a preliminary cognitive portrait to create a specific training plan that controls the choice of activities, focus zones, and the formation of difficulty. A brief pre-task questionnaire is also available that enables the performance to be observed and the variables of fatigue, sleep quality, drug timing, stress, pain and readiness. Using these inputs, the platform creates understandable signals of analysis such as fatigue-based degradation, variability of reaction-time, performance trends, and regular change summaries.
NeuroBloon, technically, is a role-based project, where the client is written in Svelte, and the server is written in FastAPI, with SQLModel to structure data and PostgreSQL to store data. The system also offers patient-facing (Bangali and English) bilingual services, secure authentication, messaging, assigning doctors to patients, notification, progress reporting, and administrative monitoring. This is because it is implemented because of NeuroBloon who provides less costly rehabilitation-based follow-up and reproducible digital health research, especially in resource-constrained settings, through the incorporation of cognitive tasks, contextual analytics, adaptive training logic and multi-role clinical coordination in a single low-cost digital platform.

\chapter{Introduction}

\section{Introduction}
Multiple sclerosis is a long-lasting neurological disorder, whose cognitive problems may be realized over time, and may change over time. Besides motor and sensory symptoms, majority of the patients experience issues in processing speed, attention, working memory, executive control and mental fatigue. These do not necessarily have to be immediate and observable on a regular clinical visit. In the majority of cases they are built up and must be followed out, in order to understand them correctly. Because of this, a mix of follow-up strategies based on the periodical assessment on site is often not adequate to identify minor yet meaningful shifts in the everyday functioning and rehabilitation needs.NeuroBloom was developed to address this need with a digital platform that encourages repeated cognitive interaction, and organized information collection, as well as a direct review by a clinician. This motivation becomes even stronger in a country like Bangladesh, where regular access to high-quality MRI, laboratory investigations, and specialist follow-up may not always be practical for many patients because of cost, distance, and resource limitations. In that setting, a low-cost digital system cannot replace formal diagnosis, but it can reduce the gap between hospital visits by supporting repeated cognitive monitoring and rehabilitation-oriented follow-up. The project is not an isolated computerized test, but a set of a combination of a baseline measurement, training, contextual questionnaires, longitudinal analytics, and patient, doctor and administrator workflows. This does not only render this system useful in cognitive exercise, but also systematic monitoring and rehabilitation support in MS-related care.

\section{Background / Problem Statement}
A series of practical problems with the current cognitive follow-up systems are the main driver of NeuroBloom. To begin with, the majority of the existing evaluations are clinic-based and are conducted in intervals. They are useful snapshots, but not necessarily suitable to low-cost monitoring, to be repeated between visits. Second, the contextual variables, such as fatigue, sleep quality, medication timing, stress, and pain, are prone to influence cognitive performance in MS unless such variables are recorded in a systematic way, daily performance fluctuation is hard to explain. Third, the rehabilitation activities may not necessarily be supplemented with clinical review and this means that the patients are able to carry out an activity of a cognitive nature and the physician has no definite method of evaluating progress and modifying recommendations, or addressing the risks.These problems are more apparent in the setting with limited resources, where such practices as repeated MRI or lab tests, or even a visit to a specialist to conduct a follow-up, may not be regularly available. In this case, a conveniently available software package that can facilitate the repetitive cognitive assessment and training process, sustain long-term records, generate analytics that can be easily understood, and connect patient-side activity to physician control is evidently needed. The resulting question to be answered by the present project is not only how to deliver cognitive tasks through the digital platform, but how to create a comprehensive workflow to oversee and assist in decision-making in multiple sclerosis, which is rehabilitation oriented.

\section{Objectives}
The main objective of this project is to design and implement a modular digital platform for long-term cognitive monitoring and clinician-guided rehabilitation in multiple sclerosis.

The specific objectives are as follows:

\begin{itemize}[leftmargin=0.6in]
\item To develop a patient-facing system that supports baseline cognitive assessment and repeated training sessions across multiple cognitive domains.
\item To build an adaptive session-planning mechanism that selects tasks, controls domain focus, and updates difficulty based on patient performance.
\item To capture contextual factors such as fatigue, sleep quality, medication timing, pain, stress, and readiness before task execution.
\item To generate understandable analytical indicators such as fatigue-related decline, reaction-time variability, long-term trends, and reliable change summaries.
\item To provide doctor-facing tools for patient assignment, progress review, intervention tracking, prescription support, and report generation.
\item To provide administrative tools for controlled deployment, role management, activity monitoring, and risk-oriented oversight.
\item To improve accessibility through a bilingual patient-facing interface in Bangla and English.
\item To create a reusable software framework that can support future research and further extension in digital cognitive rehabilitation.
\end{itemize}

\section{Scope}
A scope of NeuroBloon is design and implementation of full-stack cognitive monitoring and rehabilitation support role-based web application. The frontend of the project uses Svelte to communicate with users, FastAPI to serve the application logic and serve API, SQLModel to model the data and PostgreSQL to store the data. The modules deployed are authentication, baseline assessment, training-plan generation, adaptive difficulty control, task rotation, long-term progress tracking, pre-task contextual questionnaires, advanced analytics, doctor assignment workflows, messaging, notifications, progress reporting and administrative monitoring, and training-plan generation.The project has six cognition areas to which it is dedicated and 35 types of tasks that are registered in the platform database. It is intended to be applied in systematic monitoring, regulated rehabilitative support and research based on a digital data collection. It is not intended to replace formal neurological diagnosis, disease assessment using MRI or clinical judgment. In place of this, it is an enabling digital environment that supports the organization of recurring observation, training practice, and clinician-patient organization.

\section{Unfamiliarity of the Problem/Topic/Solution}
This project is not new because it involves the creation of a completely new neuropsychological test. The task paradigms used in NeuroBloom are extensively based on classical cognitive testing protocols and task families which are clinically well-known. The original contribution of the project is that these aspects are combined in one software system to ensure long-term MS-oriented monitoring and rehabilitation.The tests used in NeuroBloom were not selected arbitrarily. The digital games were designed by adapting established neuropsychological assessments, standard test manuals, and widely used cognitive batteries rather than inventing entirely new task logic. In practice, the project drew from recognized sources such as WAIS-IV and WMS-IV components, broader neuropsychological assessment texts, MS-focused batteries such as MACFIMS and BICAMS, and classic paradigms such as PASAT, Stroop, Flanker, Tower of London, and feature-based visual attention tasks \cite{wechsler2008,wechsler2009,lezak2012,strauss2006,benedict2002,benedict2012,gronwall1977,stroop1935,eriksen1974,shallice1982,treisman1980}. The wider task-expansion work was also informed by established paradigms such as the Wisconsin Card Sorting Test, category fluency tasks, Useful Field of View assessment, multiple object tracking, and the landmark task \cite{berg1948,henry2004,ball1993,pylyshyn1988,harvey1995}. These task families were chosen because they target cognitive domains that are commonly affected in multiple sclerosis and therefore provide a more clinically grounded basis for monitoring and rehabilitation-oriented support.NeuroBloom is not a copy of some specific application. It is an integrated platform with various features that include delivery of tasks in various areas, customized session planning, rotating tasks to prevent repetition, contextual capture of questionnaires, deterministic extraction of digital biomarkers, bilingual patient friendly, physician directed intervention and administrator control. The knowledge of literature, clinical motivation, and project-related software engineering decisions were combined to formulate the problem definition and the design of the system. That is why this project could be considered as a new system design and implementation project and not a direct copy of a specific product or a publication.

\chapter{Related Works}

\section{Introduction}
The provided work in NeuroBloom is connected to the existing research directions in the treatment of multiple sclerosis. They are clinical studies of cognitive impairments in MS, systematic screening and management guidelines, and the more recent development of digital biomarkers and digital phenotyping of monitoring the nervous system  \cite{chiaravalloti2008,rocca2015,kalb2018,insel2017,coravos2019}.  Therefore, to understand the condition of this project, one must not only take into account the popular approaches to cognitive assessment, but also the new software-based and data-driven trends that are aimed at assisting in monitoring long-term.The majority of the related work, which can be discovered in the literature, can be divided into several general categories. One of them is concerned with clinical knowledge of cognitive impairment in MS and standardized assessment battery. The other group deals with screening/management guidelines in the real clinical care. A third group deals with digital measurement, digital biomarkers and data of repeated interaction with patients. All these instructions are concepts which are borrowed by NeuroBloom but what NeuroBloom adds to these concepts is the integration of these factors into a single working software platform and not in proposing a new solitary test or a new individual measure.


\section{Related Works}
Research on cognition in multiple sclerosis shows that impairment is common and often affects processing speed, attention, working memory, and executive functioning \cite{chiaravalloti2008,amato2006,rocca2015}. These studies established the clinical importance of cognitive decline in MS, but they mainly explain the condition rather than provide a deployable software system for long-term monitoring.Another major direction is structured screening and management. BICAMS offered a practical framework for cognitive screening in MS \cite{benedict2012}, and later care recommendations emphasized routine monitoring and follow-up \cite{kalb2018}. These contributions are highly relevant, but they are mostly screening-oriented and do not provide adaptive training, contextual questionnaires, messaging, or coordinated doctor and administrator workflows.The latest literature on the topic of digital phenotyping and digital biomarkers believes that consistent digital presence can offer meaningful behavioral patterns in the long term  \cite{insel2017,dorsey2017,coravos2019}.  This line of work endorses continuous measurement, most of which is conceptual or research-based. In practice, there remains a disjunction between clinically based evaluation framework and whole software platforms that encompass patient interaction, analytics and oversight review. NeuroBloom is an invention that was developed to bridge that gap.

\section{Detailed Methodology}

\subsection{Problem Design and Analysis}
NeuroBloom was developed on the premise that cognitive change in multiple sclerosis may be gradual and immeasurable solely through isolated clinical visits only \cite{chiaravalloti2008,amato2006,rocca2015}. This issue demanded an aid of a digital system, followed, which served not only one purpose. It needed to support repetition of the task performance, history of the session over time and allow the clinicians to visualize trends rather than individual scores. Further, the MS-related performance could be influenced by fatigue, sleep quality, medication timing, stress level, and pain, thus the system needed a system to capture the contextual information as well as the cognitive outcomes.This analysis of problems has led to several design goals. Firstly, the system had to be longitudinally followed rather than tested once. Second, it had to be categorized according to clinically meaningful cognitive domains. Third, it had to generate analytical outputs that can be comprehended instead of relying on arcane prediction models. Fourth, it had to support more than one type of user as patients, physicians and administrators have different needs in a practical monitoring environment. Finally, the system should be accessible and practical in low resource environments, which motivated a web-based low-cost and bilingual system.

\subsection{Overall Framework and Architecture}
The overall Framework and Architecture is as follows:
NeuroBloom was designed as a role-based web application, and had three operating scenarios: patient, doctor and administrator. The shared backend and the database are shared by these groups of users, but each has a similar interface and a different set of responsibilities. The patient side is in charge of executing tasks and reporting on the context. The doctor side is the review of progress and summary interpretation, as well as decision-making concerning the interventions. The administrator side will engage in the system level in terms of oversight, governance and monitoring.The system is layered. The frontend was built with the help of svelte and is used to handle page rendering and to interact with tasks, localization and role-specific dashboarding. The backend was developed in FastAPI and contains authentication, session planning, storage, messaging, access to analytics, doctor workflows, and administration. SQLModel is used to do application data modeling and PostgreSQL provides persistent storage. An analytics layer processes stored session and trial records to produce longitudinal summaries and biomarker-oriented results. Such stratified isolation makes the system more maintainable and allows different modules to evolve without re-architecturing the entire system.Figure~\ref{fig:method_architecture} presents this layered role-based architecture and shows how the patient, doctor, and administrator interfaces connect to the shared backend, analytics, and database layers.
On the system level, the workflow begins with a patient registering and engages in baseline assessment tasks or proceeds with planned training tasks. The system can collect contextual information such as fatigue, sleep quality, medication time, stress, pain, and preparedness prior to the start of a session. Once the patient has completed the tasks set, the outcomes and records in context are stored in the database. These stored records are then converted to summary measurements and analysis measures. Doctors see these outputs on their dashboard and administrators have the ability to see activity and alerts system-wide. This model ensures there is a correlation of data collection, analysis and activity in one coordinated process.This end-to-end operational flow is summarized in Figure~\ref{fig:overall_workflow} and includes the engagement with the patients, a contextual capture, and storage of data, the creation of analytics and review by clinicians in one continuous process.

\subsection{Patient-Side Methodology}
There are four major steps in the patient side methodology, namely, access, assessment, training and maintenance of progress. The user gets into the access phase in which he/she logs-in to the patient-facing dashboard. The assessment of the user in the assessment phase is through the completion of baseline tasks that indicate the primary cognitive domains. The baseline is then computed as the initial results to estimate the initial cognitive profile of the user. The user undergoes four-task sessions of his/her choice during the training as per the training plan. During these sessions, the system records the measures such as score, accuracy, reaction time, completion state and contextual variables. The stored information in the final step also updates the summary of the progress and the subsequent selection of tasks.The rationale behind this methodology is that it allows repeated interaction without having the user repeat the complete process of the baseline in each interaction. It also has continuity in one session to another, which is fundamental in the long term monitoring.

\subsection{Personalized Training and Task Rotation Methodology}
The central aspect of the NeuroBloon approach is a client-centred planning (session-based). The next to be evaluated in the sequence of the patient after the baseline assessment is the six cognitive domains. The weak areas are believed to be the major area of focus, the middle areas are the secondary area of focus and the strong areas are the maintenance areas. The initial difficulty levels are allocated using the reused baseline scores and the follow-up sessions are collected with the option of the tasks among the areas of focus.The same task should not be done too often within an incredibly short period of time in that sense that there is a strategy of task rotation. This will assist in avoiding boredom, reduce the practice effects and aids in creating a more balanced activity within the library of tasks. It is a cumulative system whereby an outcome is updated with every milestone of a task made. The end of a session report provided by the system is the levels of difficulty adjustment. In this, the process helps in personalisation and development with all things not necessarily being manual.

\begin{center}
\begin{minipage}{0.92\linewidth}
\small
\textbf{Algorithm 1. Personalized session planning and task rotation in NeuroBloom}
\begin{algorithmic}[1]
\Require Baseline scores $B[d]$, recent task history $H[d]$, current difficulties $L[d]$
\State $\textit{sorted\_domains} \gets \Call{SortAscending}{B}$
\State $\textit{primary\_focus} \gets \textit{sorted\_domains}[0:2]$
\State $\textit{secondary\_focus} \gets \textit{sorted\_domains}[2:4]$
\State $\textit{maintenance} \gets \textit{sorted\_domains}[4:6]$
\ForAll{domain $d$ in $B$}
    \State $L[d] \gets \Call{ScoreToDifficulty}{B[d]}$
\EndFor
\State $\textit{session\_domains} \gets \textit{primary\_focus} \cup \textit{secondary\_focus}$
\ForAll{domain $d$ in $\textit{session\_domains}$}
    \State $\textit{available} \gets \Call{GetAvailableTasks}{d}$
    \State $\textit{recent} \gets \Call{GetRecentTasks}{H[d], d}$
    \State $\textit{candidates} \gets \Call{ExcludeRecentWhenPossible}{\textit{available}, \textit{recent}}$
    \State $\textit{strategy} \gets \Call{RotationStrategy}{d}$
    \State $\textit{task}[d] \gets \Call{SelectTask}{\textit{candidates}, \textit{strategy}, L[d]}$
\EndFor
\ForAll{completed task result $r$}
    \State \Call{StoreTrainingSession}{$r$}
    \State $L[r.domain] \gets \Call{UpdateDifficulty}{L[r.domain], r.score, r.accuracy, r.reaction\_time}$
\EndFor
\If{\Call{ShouldRebalanceFocusAreas}{active training plan}}
    \State \Call{RebalanceFocusAreas}{active training plan}
\EndIf
\State \Return personalized four-task session plan
\end{algorithmic}
\end{minipage}
\end{center}

\subsection{Contextual Data Capture and Analytics Methodology}
The contextual information and task performance are other concerns of NeuroBloom methodology. The level of fatigue, sleep quality, sleep hours, time of taking medications, pain, stress and readiness can also be collected on the site just before the beginning of a training event. These values are stored in a session-context record, and are reconciled with training-session data. The rationale behind choosing this strategy is that there could be a significant performance difference on the days of MS in executing the tasks and the cognitive scores of individuals may be inaccurate without an idea of what the external state is.Project analytics system is based on deterministic feature extraction, rather than black-box prediction. The system fails to generate many interpretable indicators that can assist the doctors and researchers to track the change in performance over time compared to the formation of opaque scores. The major indicators of the platform are the fatigue change during a session, Change in reaction-time, Smoothing of the trend using exponentially weighted moving averages and Credible change vs. baseline.Figure~\ref{fig:biomarker_pipeline_method} summarizes the full analytics pipeline, starting from contextual and task-level inputs and ending in biomarker-oriented summary measures for interpretation.

\begin{figure}[!t]
    \centering
    \includegraphics[width=\textwidth]{methodology_biomarker_analytics_pipeline}
    \caption{Biomarker analytics pipeline used in NeuroBloom.}
    \label{fig:biomarker_pipeline_method}
\end{figure}

To make the analytical design easier to understand, the main calculations are introduced below in the same order in which they are interpreted.First, the system estimates within-session change by comparing early and late parts of a task session. A reduction in accuracy and an increase in reaction time may indicate fatigue-related decline during continued task performance. These values are represented as:

\begin{equation}
\Delta Acc = Acc_{Q1} - Acc_{Q4}, \qquad \Delta RT = RT_{Q4} - RT_{Q1}
\end{equation}

Here, $Acc_{Q1}$ and $Acc_{Q4}$ represent accuracy in the early and late parts of the session, while $RT_{Q1}$ and $RT_{Q4}$ represent the corresponding reaction times.Second, the platform combines multiple components into a simple composite fatigue indicator. In this formulation, $A$ represents an accuracy-related component, $R$ represents a reaction-time-related component, and $S$ represents a self-report or symptom-related component collected from contextual input:

\begin{equation}
F = 0.5A + 0.3R + 0.2S
\end{equation}

The weighted form makes the calculation interpretable, because the relative contribution of each component is visible rather than hidden inside a black-box model.Third, the system measures response consistency using the coefficient of variation of reaction time:

\begin{equation}
CV = \frac{\sigma_{RT}}{\mu_{RT}}
\end{equation}

In this expression, $\sigma_{RT}$ is the standard deviation of reaction time and $\mu_{RT}$ is the mean reaction time. A larger value indicates more unstable responding.

Fourth, NeuroBloom uses an exponentially weighted moving average to summarize performance trends across repeated sessions while still giving more importance to recent observations:

\begin{equation}
EWMA_t = \alpha x_t + (1-\alpha)EWMA_{t-1}
\end{equation}

Here, $x_t$ is the current observation and $\alpha$ controls how strongly the newest performance affects the updated trend.Finally, the system estimates reliable change relative to baseline through the following index:

\begin{equation}
RCI = \frac{X_{\mathrm{latest}} - X_{\mathrm{baseline}}}{\sqrt{2}\,\sigma_X}
\end{equation}

The scale will identify the gap between the actual performance and the baseline performance, and scale the gap with the envisaged variability. We can then use it to tell the difference between a significant change, and normal variation.By taking these equations and combining them with each other there is no attempt to bog the reader down in mathematics. Instead, they demonstrate how the platform itself is framed so that all indicators can be interpreted clinically and that they have a purpose and are directly related to the behavior of observation in the task.

\section{Conclusion}
The NeuroBloom approach was developed to support structured and sustainable cognitive monitoring through a logical and integrated methodology. By emphasizing repeated interaction, contextual awareness, and longitudinal tracking, the methodology enables meaningful observation of cognitive changes over time rather than relying on single-session evaluation. \section{Ethical Considerations}
The ethical responsibility is enabled by such projects as NeuroBloom because the system takes into consideration human cognitive performance, health-related background and clinician-led interpretation. In the case, even though the platform was available as an academic prototype when implemented, or at least, a research-based system, it is a sensitive area anyway. The question does not concern the usage or non-usage of the code, but whether the system is fair, respectful and safe to the users.The first ethical problem is informed participation. Upon the eventuality that actual patients would be using the platform, they will be required to be aware of the type of information being gathered, the purpose of gathering the information being done, individuals who will have access to the information, and system restrictions. It is to the credit of a patient that he/she can feel relieved that such scores are not going to be used in other places, but do give answers to a physician. It is not only that the explicit consent and understandable disclosure are a formality. Well-bred system design includes them.The second ethical risk is the interpretation risk. NeuroBloon is capable of demonstrating trends, information about declines, fatigue based changes and comparisons with a baseline. These are useful extras but can be easily misinterpreted out of context. A low score will make a patient think that a reduction will occur. The interface to the system can be misinterpreted and this could result in a doctor overestimating a transitory variation. Due to this, NeuroBloom cannot be viewed as a decision-maker, rather, support system. It has to be human controlled.The other ethical dilemma is sensitive information. Fatigue, Sleep quality, when the medication is used, pain, stress, and readiness alone are hardly complex, but when combined, they demonstrate the health tendencies of individuals. This renders privacy as an ethical concern prior to becoming a legal concern. Information that does not even need to be represented in the system and information to be monitored or information that should be remembered in the system must be taken very seriously.The issue of accessibility and equity is also involved. whereby the interface can never be used to punish slowness in the response time, difficulty in reading, temporary depression or language barrier, in case the system is directed to people with neurological impairments. The ethical design in this case can be associated with the slowness in interface flow, understanding feedback rules and localized languages. The Bangli interface is not an interface feature at that. It is also an ethical advantage as it minimizes the non-inclusion.Lastly, there should be no excessive promotion of the project. It would be unethical to introduce NeuroBloom to replace diagnosis, assessment based on MRI, or the opinion of a professional neurologist. Replacing clinical knowledge with the answer role is also not as challenging, but somehow less complex: the platform will aid the organization of regular online communication and facilitate the long-term monitoring process, however, it is not a replacement.

\section{Safety Considerations}
In NeuroBloon, safety is not lacking of a physical hazard like in a mechanical or industrial system. It is highly informational as well as cognitive and operational in this case, safety. As the process aimed at in the project is expected to reach people, who may feel some level of cognitive exhaustion, the sessions should be formulated, limited and comprehensible. Short task sessions, contextual questionnaires and doctor review are the features that promote the minimalization of the risk of considering all the low-performance events as failures. The problem is that, as is, a patient who is too tired can barely be forced to have a hard session just because the system dictates it.Emotional impact is another issue that is of a safety concern. Clinical assessment ought to distinguish the temporary low readiness and the general cognitive concern as well.Another one is the safety in operation. \chapter{Addressing Complex Engineering Problems and Activities}

\section{Complex Engineering Problems Associated with the Current Project}
NeuroBloom is a multi-role digital health system that integrates patients, physicians, and administrators within a single platform. The core engineering difficulty arises from the need for many interdependent components to operate correctly at the same time. Requirement integration, longitudinal data modeling, and personalized training logic each imposed design constraints that had to be resolved consistently across the full stack. A single backend had to support three distinct user roles with different information needs, the database had to preserve long-term relational records that could support both real-time interaction and retrospective analytics, and the training engine had to balance adaptive behavior with session stability.Additional complexity came from analytics design, cross-layer synchronization, and security governance. Interpretable indicators such as trend summaries and fatigue-aware views required that metric definitions remain consistent from backend computation to frontend display. Analytics charts could silently fail if the frontend rendered before data was ready, which required careful handling of UI state and component lifecycle. Role-based access control was not a login feature but a core architectural concern, since patients, doctors, and administrators must each see only what is appropriate to their role. Taken together, these overlapping challenges confirm that NeuroBloom represents a genuinely complex engineering undertaking rather than routine software development.


\section{Complex Engineering Activities Associated with the Current Project}
Building NeuroBloom required a sustained set of engineering activities covering architecture, data modeling, integration, and validation. The project began with architectural decomposition, dividing the system into a Svelte frontend, FastAPI backend, SQLModel domain layer, and PostgreSQL database so that each layer could evolve independently. This was followed by careful schema design to ensure that users, training plans, cognitive tasks, sessions, and contextual records were linked in a way that supported both real-time operation and long-term analytics. The training engine then translated baseline performance into personalized session plans, handling domain-level difficulty, task rotation, daily limits, and cooldown logic to deliver adaptive behavior without destabilizing the patient experience.The remaining activities focused on integration, validation, and usability. Frontend-backend integration required consistent API contracts across every workflow from login and task submission to doctor review and admin analytics. Debugging and validation uncovered non-obvious failures at layer boundaries, such as empty charts caused by rendering timing issues rather than backend errors, which required systematic diagnosis rather than guessing. Synthetic demonstration data was seeded in a coherent way so that analytics dashboards reflected realistic recent activity windows. Throughout, role-specific usability was refined to ensure that each user group received an interface suited to their cognitive load and workflow needs. Together these activities demonstrate that the project required disciplined and iterative engineering effort at every stage.

\section{Conclusion}
The complexity of NeuroBloom, as has been showed in this chapter, is not a coincidence and a surface effect. It is rotated around the transparentness of the issue at hand: multi-role digital health long-term cognitive monitoring. The project also needed to have embedded adaptive task delivery, capture in terms of context, longitudinal analytics, bilingual access everywhere, role based workflows and administrative controls within one unitary coherent environment.That is why, the engineering concerns, and the engineering efforts of the project were quite significant. These problems were uncertainty, dependence and sensitivity. The activities were architectural planning, cyclic implementation, validation and coordinated inter-layer implementation. It is this and this combination that enables NeuroBloom to be a great representation of a multifaceted engineering work on contemporary software systems.

\chapter{Conclusions}

\section{Summary}
The NeuroBloom is a web application, open source, modular idea of longitudinal cognitive assessments and clinician managed rehabilitation of multiple sclerosis. Neither is it a single test nor a single dashboard that the project contributes the most. It involves installing an inter-systemic set-up in which the baseline assessment, the presence of repeated task-related interaction, contextual self-reporting, adaptive session planning, physician-oriented review and administrative control are co-occurring in one system. The platform enables easier sustained or ongoing maintenance of behavioral patterns, which are habitually ignored in the traditional episodic clinical contacts. The implemented delivery reveals that repetitive digital surveillance may be designed in a systematic, explicatory, and clinical relatively straightforward way. The detachable structure, multilingual patient support, and reusable analytic-conscious structure also contribute to it an easy of digital health study and low-cost follow-up especially in resource constrained centers where round-the-clock access to specialists may not always be available.



\section{Limitations}
Although the project was successful in meeting its software requirements, it was constrained in a number of ways. The first of them is that the given study is rather an engineering and implementation study rather than a formal clinical validation study. The system is built, tested and proven to be a working multi-role platform, but not yet tested on a large-scale longitudinal implementation with actual subjects to a controlled clinical protocol. This is why, the present report is likely to be proud of the readiness of the workflow, the analytics readiness, the maturity of implementation, yet the clinical efficacy is missing.The scope of dataset is the other limitation. It was shown on a mix of a stored operational data and organized synthetic seeded data. This proved useful to test dashboards, time-windowed analytics, and longitudinal views without exposing sensitive data about patients. However, seeded data are seeded data. They contribute in the determination of whether the system is working as it ought to but they do not replace the evidence collected on a larger sample of the population in the real world and a lengthy period of following up.The existing number of analytics is limited as well. NeuroBloom is specifically interested in explainable signal, rather than the ambiguous robots. It is a strength, and it presupposes that the current analytics layer is also conservative. The meaningful trends, variability patterns, and changes based on the context can be demonstrated, although more advanced predictive or risk-stratification has not been completely formulated or proven in the clinical setting. The system is present as it is today that assists in interpretation. It does not automatize the clinical judgment.At the level of deployment, the site is also dependent on internet connectivity, device connectivity and the degree of user experience using a web-based interface. Bilingual support reduces one of the barriers, but, not all the barriers are removed with bilingual support. Some patients may also find repetitive digital activities to be an exhausting process, both to learn and to do, and have no ability to do them regularly, especially when fatigue, vision or connection impairments prevent use. It means that quality of the systems will not be the only aspect that needs to be implemented in practice, but support, onboarding and conditions of real access.Finally, the current task library, even though it is large, is not complete. The platform also has multiple domains and a large amount of task variants, which potentially can be further expanded in the future with more varied tasks and domain specificity of the assessments. Likewise, tools facing doctors and administrators are already working and beneficial, but can be improved further based on feedback of the domain, more specific reports, and increased experience with long-term deployments.


\section{Recommendations and Future Works}
The most important follow up of NeuroBloom is large scale implementation with real users over an extended period of time. This would allow the project to transcend implementation implementation validation and more substantive evidence about the longitudinal user engagement, practical compliance, clinician acceptance, and real utility of repeat digital cognitive follow-up. Installing the system into a more extended use would also contribute to revealing the behavior under the non-controlled environment of development, and interruptions, uneven usage and different user demands have a greater prevalence.Work in the future also should be extended in terms of work in the task library. As it stands, NeuroBloon already has multiple cognitive domains and different types of tasks, but the additional variant of the tasks would increase the variety, reduce the effect of repetition and more comprehensive profile of domains. This is especially in the long-term systems. The task variety will be entailed in both the quality of science and the interaction with the patients in case one of the platforms is planned to be utilized in weeks or months.The second positive trend in the impact of the work on the future is the rich content of the analytics layer. The current platform is committed to interpretable statistical indicators, and the method is appropriate in a clinician-centered system. There can be an extension of the analytical framework, however. The resultant generation can possess stronger longitudinal prediction, increased levels of risk signals customization, higher adherence measurements, and more delicate variation of performance across its contexts. Outcome genetic extension generated should be transparent and cannot be in place of clinician-based interpretation.Even workflow with regard to facing the doctor can be extended. The intervention recording, progress-reporting, longitudinal comparison, and patient-specific rehabilitation advice systems could be improved in the future through the development of interventions.
