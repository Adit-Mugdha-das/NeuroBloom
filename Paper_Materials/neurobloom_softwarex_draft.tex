\begin{abstract}
NeuroBloom is a modular digital health platform developed to support longitudinal cognitive monitoring and clinician-guided rehabilitation workflows for people living with multiple sclerosis (MS). The system integrates patient-facing cognitive tasks, pre-session contextual questionnaires, digital biomarker extraction, clinician review tools, prescription and training-plan management, and administrative oversight within a role-based architecture. NeuroBloom was designed for repeated low-cost follow-up between formal clinical visits, with emphasis on observing subtle performance change over time rather than making diagnostic claims. By combining structured task execution with longitudinal analytics and coordinated patient-doctor workflows, the platform aims to support accessible and reproducible digital rehabilitation research and practice in MS care.
\end{abstract}

\section{Motivation and significance}
Multiple sclerosis is a chronic neurological disease in which cognitive difficulties may emerge gradually and may not be captured adequately by isolated clinical encounters. In addition to motor and sensory symptoms, many people with MS experience changes in processing speed, attention, working memory, executive control, and cognitive fatigue \cite{chiaravalloti2008,amato2006,rocca2015}. These changes often require repeated observation over time rather than one-off measurement, particularly when functional decline is subtle, fluctuating, or intertwined with contextual factors such as fatigue, sleep quality, stress, and medication timing \cite{amato2006,kalb2018}.

At the same time, longitudinal follow-up in routine practice is frequently fragmented. Formal assessments may be relatively infrequent, patient-reported experiences between visits may be poorly structured, and rehabilitation activities are often disconnected from clinician review and adjustment \cite{kalb2018}. In lower-resource environments, these challenges are intensified by limited access to specialized services and the practical cost of repeated evaluation. This creates a software gap: clinicians and patients need an accessible digital system that can support repeated cognitive follow-up, structured rehabilitation activity, and coordinated interpretation of longitudinal change without positioning itself as a diagnostic substitute for established neurological investigation.

NeuroBloom was developed to address this gap as a modular, role-based software platform for MS-oriented cognitive monitoring and rehabilitation support. The system combines patient-facing cognitive task execution, contextual session questionnaires, longitudinal session history, clinician review dashboards, prescription and training-plan workflows, and administrative oversight. Rather than treating cognitive tasks as isolated exercises, NeuroBloom organizes them as a structured task library spanning multiple domains, including processing speed, working memory, attention, executive flexibility, planning, and visual scanning. The platform standardizes outputs such as accuracy, response speed, completion behavior, and variability across sessions so that repeated interaction data can be reviewed longitudinally.

An additional motivation for the software is the growing importance of digital biomarkers in neurorehabilitation and remote monitoring research \cite{insel2017,coravos2019,dorsey2017}. NeuroBloom includes an analytics layer that derives behavioral indicators from task interaction data and contextual information, including fatigue-related patterns, response-time variability, and trend measures across repeated sessions. These outputs are intended to support clinician-guided interpretation and personalized rehabilitation planning, not to diagnose MS or replace MRI, laboratory testing, or specialist neurological judgment. This distinction is essential in order to keep the software scientifically useful while avoiding overclaiming clinical capability.

The experimental setting for NeuroBloom is therefore a coordinated digital follow-up workflow. A patient interacts with baseline or training tasks through the patient interface, optionally providing pre-session contextual information such as fatigue or sleep quality. Performance data are stored and analyzed over time, after which a clinician can review longitudinal trends, assess adherence and task-level performance, and adjust prescriptions or training plans through the doctor portal. Administrative users support governed deployment, user management, and operational oversight. In this way, the software is designed not only for repeated patient interaction but also for integration into supervised rehabilitation and monitoring workflows.

Several elements of NeuroBloom build on established neuropsychological practice, including the use of task paradigms relevant to processing speed, sustained attention, working memory, and executive function \cite{benedict2012,kalb2018}. However, the contribution of the present software is not the introduction of a new cognitive test in isolation. Instead, NeuroBloom provides a unified digital environment that links task execution, contextual capture, longitudinal analytics, clinician-guided rehabilitation, and multi-role workflow management in a single platform. This makes the software relevant to future work on accessible MS monitoring, remote rehabilitation support, digital biomarker exploration, and reproducible longitudinal cognitive follow-up in both research and practice.

\section{Software description}

NeuroBloom is implemented as a modular, web-based software platform that combines role-specific interaction, structured cognitive task delivery, longitudinal data capture, and clinician-oriented review within a unified workflow. At system level, the software connects patient-facing task execution, doctor-facing monitoring and intervention support, administrative oversight, and an analytics component that transforms repeated session records into longitudinal summaries and behavioral indicators. This design was chosen to support supervised cognitive follow-up in MS while keeping the platform extensible for additional task modules, reporting components, and future research-oriented analytics.

\subsection{Software architecture}
NeuroBloom follows a modular, role-based architecture designed to support repeated cognitive follow-up, clinician-guided rehabilitation, and governed multi-user deployment within a single web platform. The system is organized around three primary interface contexts---patient, doctor, and administrator---that share a common backend and persistent data model while exposing role-appropriate workflows. This structure allows patient interaction, clinician supervision, and operational oversight to remain integrated without collapsing their responsibilities into a single interface.

At implementation level, the software combines a Svelte-based frontend with a FastAPI backend and a structured persistence layer built around SQLModel and PostgreSQL. The frontend provides role-specific pages for task execution, progress review, patient management, analytics, and administration, while the backend exposes application services for authentication, baseline and training workflows, reporting, messaging, notifications, and doctor-admin coordination. The resulting layered design separates user interaction from service logic and data storage, which simplifies maintenance and supports extension of the platform as new task modules or workflow components are added.

Within this architecture, data flow begins when a patient performs baseline or training activities and, when applicable, submits contextual information such as fatigue, sleep quality, stress, or medication timing. These records are transferred through backend services that manage session creation, task submission, longitudinal history, and controlled access for clinician and administrator views. Persisted session data include performance summaries such as score, accuracy, response time, completion behavior, and associated contextual variables, allowing repeated observations to be reviewed over time rather than as isolated task results.

An analytics component is layered on top of these stored session records to transform repeated interaction data into longitudinal summaries and behavioral indicators. Rather than operating as a standalone diagnostic engine, this component functions as a computational monitoring pipeline that aggregates task outputs, derives variability and fatigue-related measures, evaluates change across repeated sessions, and returns interpretable summaries for clinician-facing review. This separation between interface layer, application services, analytics processing, and persistence is important for both software quality and research utility. It supports controlled role-based access, reuse of shared data structures across workflows, and incremental extension of the platform without redesigning the full system. In the context of MS-oriented monitoring and rehabilitation support, the architecture therefore serves not only as an implementation choice but also as a basis for reproducible longitudinal data capture and coordinated clinician-patient interaction.

\subsection{Software functionalities}
\paragraph{Patient interaction and session execution}
NeuroBloom provides a patient-facing workflow for repeated baseline and training activity across multiple sessions rather than one-time task use. Patients can complete structured cognitive activities, view session progress, and optionally submit pre-session contextual information such as fatigue or sleep quality before task execution. In this way, the software supports adherence-oriented longitudinal use while preserving a simple interaction flow for repeated home or clinic-based follow-up.

\paragraph{Cognitive task framework}
The task framework organizes cognitive activities as a modular library spanning domains such as processing speed, attention, working memory, executive flexibility, planning, and visual scanning. Although individual tasks differ in presentation and interaction style, they are integrated into a common session model that standardizes outputs including score, accuracy, response speed, completion behavior, and difficulty-linked progression. This allows heterogeneous task designs to contribute to a shared longitudinal record instead of remaining isolated task-specific measurements.

\paragraph{Longitudinal analytics and digital biomarkers}
Beyond raw task completion, the platform supports repeated-session monitoring through summary analytics derived from accumulated performance and contextual records. These outputs include longitudinal trend summaries, fatigue-related patterns, response-time variability, and related indicators that help characterize change across time at patient level. Their purpose within NeuroBloom is to support progression-aware monitoring and clinician interpretation of rehabilitation-relevant performance change rather than to provide diagnostic conclusions.

\paragraph{Doctor-facing clinical workflow}
The clinician-facing functionality connects repeated patient interaction data to supervised review and intervention. Doctors can inspect patient histories, assess adherence and recent performance, review biomarker-oriented summaries and trend information, generate or review progress reports, and adjust training plans or prescriptions in response to observed change. By linking monitoring outputs to concrete clinical workflow actions, the software supports iterative rehabilitation management rather than passive data display alone.

NeuroBloom also includes administrative functionality for governed deployment and operational supervision in a multi-user setting. Administrative users can review system-level activity, monitor engagement and alert-oriented summaries, manage user and role relationships, and maintain controlled oversight of the platform as a coordinated service rather than a standalone task interface. This administrative layer is important because the software is intended to function as a managed rehabilitation and monitoring environment involving patients, clinicians, and institutional oversight.

\section{Impact}
NeuroBloom contributes a research-oriented software environment for longitudinal cognitive monitoring and rehabilitation-related data collection in multiple sclerosis. By combining repeated task execution, contextual self-report capture, and persistent session history within a unified platform, the system supports structured investigation of behavioral change over time rather than isolated one-time measurement. This makes the software relevant for studies examining digital behavioral indicators, repeated cognitive interaction, and the role of contextual factors such as fatigue or sleep quality in longitudinal performance analysis \cite{insel2017,coravos2019}.

The platform also has practical value for MS-oriented follow-up between formal clinical encounters. By linking patient-side task activity with doctor-facing review tools, longitudinal summaries, and progress-oriented reporting, NeuroBloom supports repeated low-cost monitoring and clinician-guided rehabilitation workflows without positioning itself as a diagnostic substitute for established neurological assessment. In this setting, its free-of-cost software model further reduces barriers to access and use, particularly where frequent specialist review may be difficult or resource-constrained.

From a software perspective, NeuroBloom provides a modular and extensible architecture that integrates cognitive task delivery, contextual data capture, analytics, and multi-role workflow management within a single system. This design supports reuse of the platform as a digital research and deployment framework, allowing additional task modules, analytics components, and reporting workflows to be incorporated without restructuring the core architecture. NeuroBloom is therefore intended not only as an MS-oriented monitoring application, but also as reusable software infrastructure for longitudinal cognitive and rehabilitation-oriented digital health research.

\section{Conclusions}
NeuroBloom is a modular digital platform designed to support longitudinal cognitive monitoring and clinician-guided rehabilitation workflows in multiple sclerosis. By integrating patient-facing cognitive tasks, contextual data capture, longitudinal analytics, doctor-oriented review tools, and administrative oversight within a single system, the software addresses an important gap between repeated cognitive follow-up and coordinated workflow support. Its contribution lies not in proposing a standalone diagnostic instrument, but in providing reusable software infrastructure for structured monitoring, rehabilitation-oriented review, and digital biomarker exploration. Future work may extend the platform through broader deployment, additional task modules, expanded analytics, and further evaluation in research and supervised clinical settings.
